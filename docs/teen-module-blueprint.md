# EmpathiQ Teen Module Blueprint

## Why this exists

The current teen experience in the repo is still only a high-level proof of concept:

- one narrative mission
- one choice fork
- one anonymous reflection step
- no intake
- no safety gate
- no weekly loop
- no persistent progress model
- no deep Pack behavior model inside the teen surface

This document reframes the teen side of EmpathiQ as a full module, not just a single screen flow.

## What the teen module is

EmpathiQ's teen module is a preventive, mentoring-oriented emotional skills system for ages 13-18.

It is not therapy.
It is not open chat.
It is not surveillance.

It should help a teen:

- notice emotional overload early
- recognize thinking traps during school, peer, and family stress
- practice small regulation actions
- reflect safely
- feel belonging without identity exposure
- build routines that support academic function
- reach a mentor or adult when risk rises

## Research signals shaping the module

These sources directly influence the module design:

- CDC says school connectedness is associated with lower poor mental health, lower substance use and violence, and better grades, attendance, and graduation outcomes.
- WHO says adolescence is a crucial period for building sleep, coping, problem-solving, interpersonal, and emotional regulation habits, and that supportive family, school, and community environments are protective.
- WHO also notes that anxiety and depression can strongly affect school attendance and schoolwork.
- NIMH lists warning signs that need urgent escalation, including wanting to die, hopelessness, researching ways to die, withdrawing, dangerous risks, extreme mood swings, and increased drug or alcohol use.
- The U.S. Surgeon General's youth social media advisory says social media cannot currently be considered sufficiently safe for children and adolescents and recommends stronger boundaries, safer design, and effective systems for reporting harmful content.
- Apple and Google both require strong moderation for user-generated content; both are especially strict when bullying, anonymous abuse, or minors are involved.

Design implication:
the teen module must prioritize school connectedness, emotional skills, routine repair, and safe belonging, while keeping strong moderation and crisis escalation layers.

## Core product thesis

The teen module should not feel like a clinic and should not feel like a social network.

It should feel like:

- a guided narrative journey
- a quiet emotional practice space
- a structured peer mirror
- a safe bridge to adult support when needed

The central unit is not a chat thread.
The central unit is a `mission cycle`.

## Teen module pillars

### 1. Teen Intake and Safety Gate

This is the first module, before missions start.

Goals:

- collect age band, school stage, exam load, sleep pattern, screen load, home stress level, and help-seeking comfort
- identify ineligible or crisis cases before they enter standard teen missions or Packs
- route the teen into the right mission lane

Outputs:

- baseline load score
- baseline school connectedness score
- baseline family climate score
- baseline digital overload score
- risk tier
- starter mission lane

Risk tiers:

- `GREEN`: routine stress, eligible for full teen flow
- `AMBER`: moderate strain, eligible with tighter mentor review
- `RED`: high strain, limited Pack access, mandatory mentor review
- `CRISIS`: bypass standard teen flow, immediate adult escalation and referral

### 2. Daily Signal Check-In

This is the teen module's home ritual.

It should be short, repeatable, and emotionally safe.

What it captures:

- body load
- mood intensity
- sleep quality
- school pressure
- conflict at home
- social belonging
- urge to avoid, shut down, explode, or numb out

This should not become a diagnostic questionnaire.
It is a pulse system for triage and mission selection.

Output:

- today’s emotional state
- recommended mission type
- mentor visibility flag if the trend worsens

### 3. Mission Engine

This is the core of the teen module.

Each mission should be a small, structured emotional practice loop:

1. scene setup
2. body cue or sensory grounding
3. choice fork
4. thought-trap naming
5. consequence reflection
6. action experiment
7. anonymous Pack echo
8. small closure ritual

Mission families should include:

- `Exam Pressure`
- `Digital Overload`
- `Peer Belonging and Rejection`
- `Family Conflict and Tone`
- `Sleep and Recovery`
- `Decision-Making and Self-Control`
- `Confidence, Shame, and Self-Talk`
- `Identity, Boundaries, and Values`

Each mission should be tagged by:

- school stage
- stress domain
- thinking trap
- difficulty level
- required safety posture
- parent-bridge eligibility

### 4. Teen Toolbox

The teen should have a persistent support toolkit that is not tied to a single mission.

This toolbox should include:

- breath resets
- body grounding
- thought reframes
- emergency calming sequences
- exam-night routine
- conflict cool-down
- digital reset timer
- help-seeking prompts

Important rule:
toolbox items should be usable in under 2 minutes by default.

### 5. Pack Journal

The Pack is not a generic feed.
It is a structured peer reflection layer.

Teen-side rules:

- no direct messaging
- no user search
- no profile bios
- no photos
- no exact age, school, or location
- pseudonymous aliases only
- structured posting first, free text second
- reporting and hide controls always visible

Teen Pack post types:

- `What I felt`
- `What helped`
- `What I believed at first`
- `What changed`
- `One thing I want my future self to remember`

Teen reactions should stay low-risk:

- `I relate`
- `I tried this`
- `This helped`
- `Holding steady`

The Pack should reinforce:

- not being alone
- emotional normalization
- skill sharing

The Pack should not reinforce:

- identity discovery
- private alliances
- savior dynamics
- anonymous aggression
- crisis dumping without intervention

### 6. Teen Progress Model

Progress should not be based only on streaks or volume.

The module should measure:

- emotional flexibility
- routine recovery
- sleep repair
- school engagement
- help-seeking willingness
- reduced trap frequency
- improved conflict recovery
- improved digital boundaries

Visible teen progress should emphasize:

- effort
- noticing
- trying again
- recovery after rupture

Not:

- leaderboard status
- public comparison
- shame-based compliance

### 7. Mentor Bridge

The teen module should quietly feed the mentor system.

Mentors should see:

- mission completion patterns
- top thinking traps
- rising strain domains
- Pack-level trend summaries
- drop-off risk
- crisis flags

Mentors should not need raw teen posts by default.

Mentor intervention triggers:

- repeated catastrophizing under exam load
- repeated family-conflict missions with worsening reflections
- sharp decline in sleep or school engagement signals
- digital-overload pattern with social withdrawal
- substance-risk language
- self-harm or suicidal warning signs

### 8. Parent Bridge

The teen module should connect to parents carefully and indirectly.

Parents should receive:

- plain-language themes
- “what helps” suggestions
- calm-tone prompts
- small home actions

Parents should not receive:

- raw teen journal text
- raw Pack posts
- detailed emotional confessions
- surveillance-style usage logs

The teen module should decide which mission outcomes are:

- teen-only
- mentor-visible
- parent-safe summary eligible

## The weekly teen loop

The teen module should run on a weekly rhythm, not isolated sessions.

### Monday to Friday

- 30-second daily signal check
- one recommended mission every 1-2 days
- optional toolbox use
- one Pack reflection after selected missions

### Saturday

- weekly reflection
- progress replay
- “what changed this week” summary

### Sunday

- reset ritual
- next-week intention
- optional parent bridge prompt

This creates continuity without overwhelming the teen.

## Teen state model

The teen module should move through recognizable states:

- `Onboarding`
- `Baseline Mapping`
- `Mission Active`
- `Reflection Pending`
- `Pack Eligible`
- `Mentor Watch`
- `Parent Bridge Active`
- `Recovery Mode`
- `Escalated Support`

This state model matters because not every teen should see the same UI or the same level of Pack access.

## Mission taxonomy in depth

### Academic overload lane

Use when the teen shows:

- exam panic
- hopelessness about grades
- avoidance
- tuition fatigue
- boredom plus pressure

Mission goals:

- separate marks from identity
- build study pacing
- reduce catastrophic thinking
- protect sleep
- restore control over the next small step

### Family climate lane

Use when the teen shows:

- shouting at home
- blame cycles
- emotional distance
- fear of correction
- no safe place to talk

Mission goals:

- emotional naming
- tone recovery
- boundary language
- calm exit skills
- mentor escalation if the home feels unsafe

### Digital overload lane

Use when the teen shows:

- endless scrolling
- sleep displacement
- gaming avoidance
- comparison distress
- post-phone emotional crash

Mission goals:

- notice triggers
- reduce compulsive loops
- build offline recovery rituals
- strengthen analog attention

### Belonging and identity lane

Use when the teen shows:

- shame
- exclusion
- social comparison
- confusion about self-worth
- fear of being misunderstood

Mission goals:

- reduce mind-reading
- increase grounded self-story
- improve help-seeking
- connect belonging to real support, not performance

### Decision and impulse lane

Use when the teen shows:

- regret after acting
- public speaking fear
- limited problem-solving
- conflict reactivity
- risky peer-following

Mission goals:

- pause between feeling and action
- build choice awareness
- strengthen middle-ground thinking

## Safety architecture for the teen module

This module touches self-harm, bullying, family conflict, substance language, and anonymous peer content.
So the safety architecture is part of the product, not a later add-on.

### Teen safety rules

- no AI “therapy” voice
- no diagnosing language to the teen
- no crisis content published directly into the Pack
- no streak pressure after distress disclosure
- no manipulative notifications

### Automatic safety interrupts

If the teen indicates or writes content linked to:

- wanting to die
- self-harm urges
- researching suicide
- feeling trapped with no reason to live
- increased substance use
- dangerous risk-taking
- severe abuse risk

Then the module should:

- stop the normal mission loop
- show a calm, immediate support screen
- encourage reaching a trusted adult
- route to mentor or admin safety queue
- present local crisis options appropriate to deployment geography

Important:
this is based on NIMH warning-sign guidance and should be implemented as a safety workflow, not a diagnosis.

### Pack moderation rules

The teen module must support:

- report content
- hide content
- temporary posting holds
- escalation queue
- abusive-user blocking at the system level

This is required both for real safety and for app-store compliance.

## Content design rules for teen missions

Every mission should follow these principles:

- start concrete, not abstract
- use real-life school or family moments
- keep language plain, not clinical
- use sensory grounding before cognitive reframing
- make the final action small and offline-capable
- let the teen leave with relief or clarity, not a lecture

Avoid:

- overexplaining psychology
- therapist tone
- fake inspirational quotes
- shame or compliance framing
- reward systems that push daily overuse

## What is special about EmpathiQ's teen module

The differentiation is not “we have teen content.”

The differentiation is:

- narrative missions instead of lesson pages
- REBT-style trap detection embedded in choices
- a Pack that behaves like a protected reflection circle, not social media
- parent and mentor bridges that are structured and privacy-aware
- academic function treated as part of emotional support, not separate from it
- family climate and school connectedness treated as product outcomes

## Development roadmap for the teen module

### Phase 1. Teen foundation

Build:

- intake
- baseline mapping
- daily signal check
- mission engine shell
- teen toolbox v1
- safety interrupt framework

Definition of done:

- a teen can onboard, complete a signal check, receive a mission, and safely exit or escalate

### Phase 2. Mission library

Build:

- 5 mission lanes
- 8-12 missions per lane
- mission authoring template
- thinking-trap taxonomy and tagging
- consequence and action-experiment templates

Definition of done:

- the system can recommend missions based on strain type and prior trap patterns

### Phase 3. Pack v1

Build:

- structured anonymous reflections
- low-risk reactions
- moderation queue
- report and hide controls
- risk-based posting holds

Definition of done:

- teens can feel peer belonging without open-chat risk

### Phase 4. Mentor and parent bridges

Build:

- teen trend summaries for mentors
- parent-safe summary outputs
- sideways invitations for home action
- escalation routing for high-risk teens

Definition of done:

- the teen module influences the wider support system without breaching privacy

### Phase 5. Outcomes and personalization

Build:

- mission recommendation engine
- weekly summaries
- recovery-mode flows
- school connectedness and academic function tracking
- dropout-risk detection

Definition of done:

- the module adapts to the teen over time and shows measurable change

## Immediate next-step build slice

If we start this now, the strongest first slice is:

1. teen intake and safety gate
2. daily signal check
3. mission engine v1
4. toolbox v1
5. Pack journal v1 with moderation
6. mentor visibility rules

That is the smallest meaningful product slice.

## Open design decisions

These still need product decisions before implementation:

- whether Pack aliases rotate weekly or per mission cycle
- how much of the weekly summary the teen sees versus only mentors
- whether parent bridges require teen acknowledgement for non-safety summaries
- whether school-connectedness prompts stay optional or become part of baseline
- how often the teen gets nudged without the app feeling like homework
